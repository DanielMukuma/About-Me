My Research

Theme: A Comparative Institutional Analysis of the "Human Capital Pipeline" in Chinese and Japanese Public Diplomacy [cite: 3]

Background and Problem Statement

Human capital is a raw resource that requires a "refining" process to yield economic utility[cite: 5]. In Zambia, Japan possesses high-grade resources in ABE Initiative Alumni[cite: 6]. However, while the Chinese model (Confucius Institute) operates as a "Closed Loop" System—integrating training directly into the labor needs of Chinese SOEs—the Japanese model remains an "Open Loop"[cite: 7]. This is a Governance Failure because the State extracts and processes the education resource but lacks the Intermediary Organizations needed to re-integrate talent into the economic pipeline[cite: 8]. Consequently, this results in "resource leakage," where Japanese-trained talent is underutilized or lost to competitors[cite: 9].

The "Translation Gap" in Hito-zukuri

A key focus of my research is the "Translation Gap" in Hito-zukuri (Making People)[cite: 10, 12]. Vernacular concepts become "untranslatable" when decoupled from their origin[cite: 11]. In Japan, Hito-zukuri implies a social contract of long-term employment [cite: 12], whereas in Zambia, it is interpreted as mere "credentialing" for individual mobility[cite: 13]. As a result, Japanese SMEs face prohibitive transaction costs because they lack the institutional "translators" to navigate this semantic gap[cite: 14].

Research Questions

  • What specific institutional barriers prevent Japanese SMEs from utilizing ABE graduates as "Market Navigators"? [cite: 16]
  • How do the "exit strategies" in the charter documents of JICA vs. Hanban differ regarding human capital re-integration? [cite: 17]
  • Can the University serve as the missing Intermediary Organization to lower these transaction costs? [cite: 18]

Methodology

My methodology includes Institutional Mapping through a comparative audit of JICA and Confucius Institute policy frameworks[cite: 20]. I will also conduct an "Intermediary" Audit using semi-structured interviews with JICA coordinators and SME stakeholders [cite: 21], and apply Semantic Analysis to categorize policy terms that fail to translate into local economic practice[cite: 22].
